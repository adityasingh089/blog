Social Engineering

The art of manipulating people into performing actions or divulging confidential information, often for malicious purposes. Unlike hacking, which typically involves exploiting technical vulnerabilities, social engineering exploits human psychology and trust to gain unauthorized access to systems, data, or physical spaces. Digital forensics is the process of identifying, preserving, analyzing, and documenting digital evidence. This is done in order to present evidence in a court of law when required.

There are various techniques of social engineering, including:

The action performed right after the occurrence of a security incident is known as the first response. It is highly dependent on the nature of the incident.
Under this phase, the professionals search for the devices involved in carrying out the crime. These devices then carefully seized to extract information out of them.
After the search and seizure phase, professionals use the acquired devices to collect data. They have well-defined forensic methods for evidence handling.
The forensic staff should have access to a safe environment where they can secure the evidence. They determine if the collected data is accurate, authentic, and accessible.

Sending fraudulent emails, text messages, or other forms of communication that appear to be from a legitimate source, aiming to trick recipients into revealing sensitive information like passwords or financial details.
Phishing is a form of cyber attack in which the attacker impersonates a legitimate entity or organization to trick individuals into providing sensitive information such as login credentials, financial data, or personal details. Phishing attacks are typically carried out via email, although they can also occur through other communication channels such as text messages, social media, or instant messaging platforms.

Creating a fabricated scenario to obtain personal information from individuals. For example, a pretexter might pose as a bank representative and call someone to request account information under the guise of a security check.

Offering something enticing, like a free USB drive or software download, which contains malware or malicious code. When the victim interacts with the bait, their system gets compromised.

Physically following someone into a restricted area without proper authorization, often by pretending to be an employee or someone who belongs there.

Offering a favor or service in exchange for sensitive information or access. For instance, a social engineer might offer IT support in exchange for login credentials.
